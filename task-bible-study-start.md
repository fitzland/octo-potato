# Task: Prepare Adult Bible Study Lesson

This document outlines the standard workflow for preparing adult Bible study lessons that are expository and discussion-driven. The process ensures consistency unless genre adaptations are required.

All output must strictly adhere to the formatting rules in [style-bible-study.md](./style-bible-study.md).

## Teaching Philosophy & Assumptions

- Scripture is authoritative, sufficient, and internally coherent.
- Teaching explains what the text means, not personal opinions.
- Sound interpretation precedes good application.
- Scripture interprets Scripture.
- Lessons engage **mind** (understanding), **heart** (conviction), and **will** (obedience).
- Discussion serves the text; the text does not serve discussion.

## Interpretive Guardrails (Non-Negotiables)

- Do **not** build doctrine on isolated verses, narrative descriptions treated as commands, or symbolic imagery without clear anchors.
- Always ask: What did this mean to the original audience? How does the broader canon clarify it?
- Distinguish clearly between:
  - **Interpretation** — what the text means
  - **Reflection** — how the truth affects the heart
  - **Application** — how believers obey in response
- Avoid speculation, over-allegorization, moralism, or disconnected applications.

## Christological Integration Requirement

Every lesson must explicitly show how the passage points to Christ, the gospel, or life under the New Covenant (direct fulfillment, typology, contrast, theological trajectory).

## Critical Distinction: Four Types of Lesson Prompts

(See [style-bible-study.md](./style-bible-study.md) for exact formatting rules.)

1. **Discussion Questions (Mind & Community)** — Open-ended, text-focused questions for group interaction (labeled `(Discussion:Mind)`).
2. **Reflection Questions (Heart)** — Personal, introspective, silent questions for private conviction (labeled `(Reflection:Heart)`).
3. **Decision Questions (Will)** — Rhetorical calls to internal commitment, only in Closing.
4. **Application Challenges (Practice)** — Concrete, weekly actions, only in Closing (max 4, last is memory verse).

This progression (discussion → reflection → decision → practice) is intentional and must not be mixed.

## Core Workflow (Didactic Passages — Default)

Adapt for genre as shown in the table below.

### Phase 1: Method Review

Confirm passage, genre, interpretive approach, and complete thought unit.

### Phase 2: Foundational Exegesis

**Step 1 — Read & Observe**  
Read the passage (BSB) multiple times. Note repeated words, contrasts, connectives, surprises.

**Step 2 — Analyze Structure**  
Identify thought units, rhetorical flow, logical progression (commands → reasoning → conclusions).

**Step 3 — Deep Dive**  
- Linguistics: key terms (semantic range via lexicons).  
- Cross-references: canonical connections.  
- Context: historical, cultural, literary setting and audience problem.

### Phase 3: Thematic Synthesis

**Step 4 — Define Core Message**  
Distill one clear, transferable, single-sentence governing idea.  
Create:  
- Memorable **Lesson Title**  
- **Lesson Flow** outline (2–4 major section titles showing how the Core Message unfolds)

### Phase 4: Lesson Architecture

**Step 5 — Divide & Title Sections**  
Break passage into 2–4 logical sections. Give each a clear, descriptive title.

**Step 6 — Build Each Section**  
For each section:  
- Brief one-sentence focus summary  
- ### Scripture heading + blockquote (BSB, bold verse numbers)  
- ### Context & Insights (bulleted key explanations, illustrations, terms)  
- One or more ### [Topic] (Discussion:Mind) blocks with questions in > and **Bold Label**. insights  
- ### [Topic] (Reflection:Heart) with personal question in >  
- *Transition*. [italicized bridge to next section]

**Step 7 — Craft Introduction**  
- ### Icebreaker: 1–2 questions in > blockquotes  
- **Synthesis**. [paragraph tying icebreaker to theme]  
- Core Message (from Step 4)

**Step 8 — Prepare Closing**  
- ### Decision (Will): 2–3 bold-labeled commitment questions  
- ### Challenges (Practice): 2–4 bold-labeled actions (last = **Memory Verse**)  
- ### Prayer: multi-paragraph, theme-reflecting prayer

**Step 9 — Add Illustrations (Optional)**  
Insert brief, clarifying stories or metaphors only where they illuminate the text (do not force).

### Phase 5: Final Review & Assembly

**Step 10 — Verify Style Compliance**  
Before finalizing, check every element against [style-bible-study.md](./style-bible-study.md): headings, prompt labels, formatting, italics, punctuation placement, no more than 4 challenges, etc.

**Step 11 — Theological & Gospel Check**  
Confirm Christological connection is explicit and grace-driven (not moralistic).

Final Draft Check: Ensure no "Key Insights" bullets remain; all interpretive content must be delivered via Observe & Discuss questions. Use verse refs without spaces (v.8, vv.9-10). Output complete lessons in a markdown fenced code block.

## Genre-Specific Adaptations

The Bible contains multiple literary genres, each requiring slight adjustments to the standard workflow (observe → interpret → reflect → apply) to honor how the original author communicated. Always start with text-first observation questions, then move to interpretive/discovery questions. Maintain Socratic priority: lead participants to discover truths through questions, not declarations.

Use these adaptations when the passage is primarily one genre (note: many books mix genres—identify the dominant one in the section).

### Narrative (e.g., Genesis–Esther, Gospels, Acts)
- **Goal**: Reveal God's character, redemptive plan, and faith patterns through story (plot, characters, conflict/resolution).
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Focus on plot elements: central conflict, rising action, climax, resolution.
  - Character analysis: What do actions/words reveal about people and God?
  - Descriptive, not prescriptive: Avoid turning examples into commands.
  - Core Message: What does this story show about God? Humanity? Faith/sin?
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What is the main conflict here? How does the character respond (v.5)?
  - Interpret: What does this action reveal about God's character (v.12)?
  - Apply: How is my situation like this character's? What faith pattern should I emulate or avoid?

### Law (e.g., Exodus–Deuteronomy, Leviticus)
- **Goal**: Show God's holiness, justice, and covenant relationship; point to Christ as fulfillment.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Categorize: moral (timeless), ceremonial (worship/purity), civil (society).
  - Ask "why" behind commands: What does this reveal about God?
  - Bridge to Christ: How is this fulfilled/transformed in the New Covenant?
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What specific command is given here (v.3)?
  - Interpret: Why was this law needed in Israel's context (v.7)?
  - Apply: How does Christ fulfill this principle today (cross-ref to Hebrews)?

### Poetry / Songs (e.g., Psalms, Song of Solomon, Lamentations)
- **Goal**: Express emotion, shape imagination, give voice to worship/lament before God.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Focus on parallelism (synonymous, antithetic, synthetic), imagery, emotional arc (e.g., lament → trust → praise).
  - Treat as formative for heart/prayer, not always propositional.
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What images or repeated words stand out (v.4)?
  - Interpret: How does the parallelism build the emotion here (vv.5-6)?
  - Apply: How can this Psalm shape my prayers in suffering or joy?

### Wisdom (e.g., Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes)
- **Goal**: Teach skillful living in a fallen world; principles, not ironclad promises.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Recognize aphorisms, observations, dialogues (e.g., Job's debates).
  - Avoid treating proverbs as guarantees—general truths.
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What contrast is drawn here (v.10)?
  - Interpret: What life principle does this observation teach?
  - Apply: How can this wisdom shape my decisions this week?

### Prophecy (e.g., Isaiah–Malachi)
- **Goal**: Call to repentance, reveal consequences, promise restoration; pattern often indictment → judgment → hope.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Anchor in historical context: Who? When? Why?
  - Look for fulfillment in Christ or New Covenant.
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What sin is indicted here (v.2)?
  - Interpret: What hope or promise follows judgment (vv.10-12)?
  - Apply: How does this call to repentance speak to our culture today?

### Apocalyptic (e.g., Daniel, Revelation)
- **Goal**: Encourage suffering believers with visions of God's ultimate victory; heavy symbolism rooted in OT.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Trace symbols to OT roots; focus on big theology (sovereignty, worship, endurance).
  - Avoid over-decoding every detail—emphasize main message.
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What key symbol or vision is described (v.13)?
  - Interpret: How does this echo earlier OT imagery (cross-ref)?
  - Apply: How does this vision encourage faithfulness now?

### Epistles / Letters (e.g., Romans–Jude)
- **Goal**: Teach doctrine and practice in specific church contexts.
- **Key Adjustments**:
  - Follow logical argument flow, commands, contrasts.
  - Note occasion and recipients.
- **Question Examples**:
  - Observe: What command or contrast does Paul make (v.5)?
  - Interpret: Why does he emphasize this truth here?
  - Apply: How does this doctrine change my daily life?

### General Rule
- Always identify the dominant genre early (in Introduction or first Observe questions).
- Adapt question focus but keep Socratic flow: observe text → discover meaning → reflect personally → apply in Christ.
- Cross-reference to Christ/gospel in every lesson (e.g., "How does this point forward to Jesus?").

This ensures genre-honoring interpretation without speculation or moralism.

## Final Reminders

- Text drives the lesson.
- Explicit gospel/Christ connection required.
- Reflection during teaching; application at end.
- Grace, not performance, is the goal.
- **Always verify final lesson against style-bible-study.md before considering it complete.**
